How Australians think about same-sex marriage, mapped

By Matt Liddy and Nathan Hoad

Posted August 14, 2017 13:45:38

As preparations begin for a voluntary national survey on same-sex marriage, there's a lot we already know about Australians' views.

Polling consistently shows a majority of Australians support same-sex marriage. A year ago, the ABC's Vote Compass asked Australians whether they agreed or disagreed with this statement:

Marriage should only be between a man and a woman.

More than 1.2 million Australians responded, and a majority of people indicated support for same-sex marriage.

External Link: Chart: Majority of voters supportive of same-sex marriage

Keep in mind:

  • Yes, this data is 12 months old, but it remains the most detailed nationwide snapshot of voters' views on same-sex marriage.
  • Recent evidence suggests Australian opinion on same-sex marriage is only moving in one direction.

Typical opinion polls don't have a large enough sample size to provide data on a seat-by-seat basis. Vote Compass does. So let's see what that looks like.

On this map, every electorate is shaded based on voters' support for same-sex marriage: blue seats are less supportive and purple seats are more supportive.

One of the defining trends of Australian politics clearly holds true on this issue: Queensland is our most conservative state and Victoria the most progressive.

But this bird's eye view doesn't tell us the whole story: it over-emphasises a handful of large regional electorates, while the much smaller electorates in our capital cities remain basically invisible.

That matters because the cities are much less conservative than regional areas. Sydney, for instance, provides a very different picture of views on same-sex marriage.

This cluster of Sydney seats represents more voters than the large electorates that dominated our initial field of view — and support for same-sex marriage is above 50 per cent across most of the city.

In Prime Minister Malcolm Turnbull's seat, Wentworth, a large majority of 76 per cent of voters indicated support for same-sex marriage.

Mr Turnbull is encouraging Australians to vote 'yes' in the planned postal vote, supporting change to allow gay couples to marry.

In the neighbouring electorate of Warringah, 66 per cent of voters opposed the idea that marriage should only be between a man and a woman. But the local member here holds a different view.

This is former prime minister Tony Abbott's seat, and he has already made clear he will be a leading advocate for the 'no' campaign.

One of the strongest Liberal voices backing same-sex marriage under both Mr Turnbull's and Mr Abbott's watch has been Queenslander Warren Entsch.

About half of voters in Mr Entsch's north Queensland seat indicated their support for same-sex marriage, compared to 39 per cent who said marriage should be only between a man and a woman.

Elsewhere in regional Queensland is the seat where voters are the least supportive of same-sex marriage out of anywhere in the nation, Maranoa.

But even here support for same-sex marriage is 32 per cent, while 54 per cent of voters say marriage should be only between a man and a woman.

Out of the 10 seats in Australia with the lowest support for same-sex marriage, seven are in Queensland.

But again, in the inner-city, it's another story. The Member for Brisbane, Trevor Evans, was a high-profile voice alongside Mr Entsch in pushing for the Liberal party room to change its position.

A majority of voters in Mr Evans's seat back his view.

But support is not as strong in Brisbane's middle and outer suburbs. Forde, south of the city, is one of the 10 suburbs most opposed to same-sex marriage in the country.

To find the seat with the strongest backing for same-sex marriage in Australia, we need to head out of Queensland and take a look at Melbourne.

Labor MP David Feeney's seat of Batman is the most pro-SSM seat in the country, with 79 per cent of voters indicating their support. Only 15 per cent here say marriage should be only between a man and a woman.

Again, that's in line with Victoria's position as Australia's most politically progressive state — seven of the top 10 seats in favour of same-sex marriage are in Victoria.

Opposition Leader Bill Shorten's seat, Maribyrnong, is not on that top 10 list but a clear majority of voters back his position in support of same-sex marriage.

In Tasmania, city areas are again the strongest supporters of same-sex marriage.

In Braddon, 46 per cent of voters indicate they're opposed to same-sex marriage, compared to 43 per cent in support.

The geographic trends holds up in South Australia too, with the inner-city broadly in support.

And regional areas less supportive, with the seat of Grey among the country's 10 seats least supportive of same-sex marriage.

And Perth.

Darwin is less clear because of the small number of NT seats.
